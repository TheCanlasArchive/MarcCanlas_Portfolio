My Journey as an IT Web Developer

From a boarding house room and a blank Python file, to building full websites, a personal account of how it all started in 2023.

It started in 2023. I was new to everything, a new course, new environment, new city. I was renting a boarding house room, far from home, trying to figure out what being an IT student actually meant.

The rough start: Python and a new life

The first language they put in front of me was Python. I had no prior programming experience worth mentioning, and honestly, the language itself wasn't the hardest part. The hardest part was everything around it adjusting to living alone, navigating a new place without a familiar face, and sitting in front of a screen trying to make sense of syntax errors when I had nobody to ask.

There were moments when it felt genuinely overwhelming. The kind of overwhelm where you don't just struggle with the subject you question whether you belong there at all. But I kept showing up. I studied the way I always had: diligently, quietly, on my own pace. Not the fastest in the room, but consistent.

The turning point: HTML and CSS

Then came HTML and CSS, and something shifted.

I had actually been introduced to both back in international school, just briefly, just enough to know what a tag looked like. But now I was building with them properly, and it clicked in a way Python hadn't. I could see what I was making. A line of code would produce something on screen, and that immediate feedback felt completely different from debugging logic in a terminal.

I started using PowerPoint as a design scratchpad. I know that sounds unconventional, but it gave me a space to sketch layouts, play with color combinations, and think visually before writing a single line of code. It was how I translated the design ideas in my head into something I could reference while building. That habit of thinking about design first has stuck with me.

Expanding the stack, one difficult thing at a time

The curriculum kept moving. Each semester brought something new to learn, and the pace didn't slow down to let you fully digest one thing before the next arrived.

  • PHP and SQL — backend logic, databases, making things actually work under the hood
  • Vue.js with MongoDB — a different way of thinking about how a UI behaves
  • Angular — structured, opinionated, a whole framework to internalize
  • Flutter — mobile territory, a different paradigm altogether

There were moments where the material piled up faster than I could process it, but I tried to take it own my pace to understand it slowly and clearly.

Where I am now

I'm still learning and exploring, and that's one of the things I enjoy most about this field. Through building projects and solving real-world challenges, I've developed a stronger foundation in both development and problem-solving. Every experience teaches me something new, and I'm excited to keep growing, creating, and discovering what's next.
