The Way I Design: Picky, Patient, and Always Revising

Hours of hunting for the right reference, dozens of revisions, in search of the right reference. this is my version of design looks like.

Whenever a professor assigned something design-related, a presentation, a layout, anything visual, I never sat down and just started. I went looking first. Sometimes for a long time.

The resource hunt

I end up with a dozen tabs open: Unsplash, Pinterest, random design blogs, and portfolios I’ve bookmarked. It’s not because I’m unsure of what I want; it’s because I need to see what’s out there to realize what I don’t want. It’s a process of elimination that helps me find my footing.

It takes time, and I know that sometimes makes me slower to start than others. But I think it’s worth it. If you rush, you end up building the first idea that popped into your head, and for me, the first idea is almost never the right one.

The assumption problem

Before you start building, you have this perfect version of the design in your head. But that version is just a collection of assumptions. You assume a certain color will work, or that a specific layout will feel balanced.

Then you build it, and it never looks the way you imagined. That’s when the real work happens. You move things around, swap out fonts, change the palette, and effectively break it down to build it back up. It’s messy, and it’s definitely not the linear process we’re often told to follow. I’ve made dozens of versions of a single slide, not because I'm indecisive, but because every "wrong" version gives me more information about what actually works.

Still growing, still exploring

I don’t think there’s a "final" version of my design style. You don’t just stop growing once you find a look that works. In fact, I think real growth happens when you deliberately step into the techniques or tools you've been avoiding. There are styles out there that feel a bit uncomfortable or unfamiliar, and that’s exactly why I need to spend time with them.

Staying comfortable is easy, but it’s the quickest way to stop evolving. I want to keep exploring the things I don't use yet.
